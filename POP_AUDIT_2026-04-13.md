# City Bubble Population Audit

**Date:** April 13, 2026
**Scope:** City centroid population bubbles (`rollupByCity` → `cityRollupToCentroidGeoJSON`)
**Ground truth:** ACS 2022 5-year estimates (table B01003_001E)
**Source file under audit:** `ui/src/assets/cities-coastal.json` (4,329 coastal cities, 18 states)

## Top-line finding

**The bubble populations are trustworthy.** A stratified sample of 32 cities across 12 coastal states shows a median drift of **−0.22%** and a mean of **+0.76%** vs ACS 2022 5-year. 81% of sampled cities match ACS within ±2%, 97% within ±10%.

No systematic bias; no stale or swapped data. The one cluster worth noting is small CDPs (<5k population), where ACS sampling error naturally grows.

## Provenance

`cities-coastal.json` is **Decennial 2020 Census** counts, not ACS. Verified by exact-match on marker cities:

- New York: 8,804,190 = Decennial 2020 ✓
- Houston: 2,304,580 = Decennial 2020 ✓
- Charlotte: 874,579 = Decennial 2020 ✓

Decennial 2020 and ACS 2022 5-year share a mid-2020 centroid, so differences here reflect real population change (2020 → ~2022) plus ACS sampling variance, not methodology mismatch.

## Sample results

32-city stratified sample; states weighted toward hurricane-exposed coast (TX, FL, LA, AL, MS, GA, SC, NC, VA, NY, NJ, MD, MA). Size tiers: 1 XL (>500k), 5 L (100–500k), 6 M (20–100k), 6 S (5–20k), 4 XS (<5k), plus organic top-tier picks.

| Within         | Count   | Share    |
| -------------- | ------- | -------- |
| ±2%            | 26 / 32 | **81%**  |
| ±5%            | 28 / 32 | 88%      |
| ±10%           | 31 / 32 | 97%      |

- **Mean drift:** +0.76% (ACS slightly higher)
- **Median drift:** −0.22%
- **Range:** −5.7% (Greenbriar, VA) to +18.3% (Indian Hills, TX)

## Where drift shows up

### Large cities: slight post-COVID undercount in the bubbles
ACS 2022 5-year captures the 2020–2022 outmigration from dense urban cores that Decennial 2020 didn't yet see. Bubble populations read ~1–2% high for:

- New York, NY: −2.1% (−181,723)
- Boston, MA: −1.4%
- Mount Vernon, NY: −1.5%
- Buffalo, NY: −0.6%

Even the largest absolute delta (NYC −182k) is a 2% effect. None of this changes which cities rank as high-priority.

### Small CDPs: ACS sampling noise dominates
Places with <5,000 population carry large margins of error in ACS (routinely ±15–25%). Observed outliers are consistent with ACS noise, not bad source data:

- Indian Hills, TX: +18.3% (bubble 2,694 → ACS 3,188)
- Meraux, LA: +7.2% (bubble 6,804 → ACS 7,297)
- Clarkson, NY: +7.0% (bubble 4,694 → ACS 5,022)
- Greenbriar, VA: −5.7% (bubble 8,421 → ACS 7,945)

These are small-sample ACS artifacts; the Decennial 2020 counts are actually closer to ground truth for these CDPs than ACS 2022 would be. I'd recommend **not** migrating the dataset to ACS for this tier — you'd trade exact counts for sampling error.

### Mid-tier cities: boringly accurate
Cities 20k–500k track ACS within 1% almost universally. Tyler TX, McAllen TX, Mobile AL, Charlottesville VA, Alexandria LA, Perth Amboy NJ, Roanoke VA all land within ±1%.

## Recommendations

1. **Keep Decennial 2020 as the source.** It's the right choice for accuracy, especially for the small-city tier where ACS would add noise. The audit shows the bubbles are behaving correctly.

2. **Consider annotating vintage in the UI.** Bubbles currently show raw population without a "(2020)" tag. For responders making calls in 2026, a small "2020 Census" chip in the bubble popover would set the right expectation that NYC's number hasn't been updated for post-COVID shifts.

3. **Optional future refresh:** When Census releases **Decennial 2030** (expected 2031) or if you want mid-decade accuracy, **PEP (Population Estimates Program) vintage 2024 or 2025** is the better update target than ACS — PEP is a full-count estimate (not a sample), so it doesn't introduce ACS noise for small CDPs.

4. **No action needed on the rollup logic itself.** `rollupByCity` in `jurisdictions.ts` assigns buildings to cities via a spatial grid, then sums — there's no population math on the JS side that could introduce drift. The `pop` field on each `CityEntry` is passed through verbatim from the source JSON.

## What I didn't audit (out of scope)

- County centroid bubbles (separate source: `counties-coastal.json`)
- `DashboardPanel` `estimatedPop` — computed from building counts × persons-per-household, separate pipeline
- CAT / SitRep report population figures
- The spatial assignment of buildings → cities (could be a source of apparent "pop drift" at the bubble level even with correct source data, if cities have ambiguous boundaries)
