to StormDPS, which forecasts storm destructive potential before

maximum-of-maximums surge over public building footprints, and gives
CAT leads and emergency managers a first-pass read on damage hotspots,
peril mix, and deployment sizing — all from free .gov APIs, no
proprietary data.

The piece I'd love your gut check on is the adjuster-per-day
throughput table. I picked conservative numbers deliberately (rule: an
honest underestimate that sends a 15-person team is better than an
optimistic estimate that sends 10 and falls behind by day 3), but
they're rule-of-thumb only until a real CAT manager tells me they're
plausible.

Here are the current numbers SurgeDPS uses to size a deployment. They
represent how many inspections one adjuster can complete per 8-hour
day for each severity tier, and they drive the "X adjusters × Y days"
recommendation and the time-to-clear estimate.

    Severity   Per-adjuster-day   Raw rule-of-thumb I started from
    --------   ----------------   ---------------------------------
    Severe              7                       15
    Major              12                       25
    Moderate           20                       40
    Minor              30                       60

"Severe" means the building is likely uninhabitable — collapse, major
structural damage, or 6+ ft of surge through the first floor. "Major"
is significant structural damage but the shell is intact. "Moderate"
is mostly contents, drywall, flooring, mechanicals. "Minor" is nuisance
flooding, cosmetic, roof tab loss.

The numbers in the first column are what SurgeDPS ships with today
(deliberately halved from the rule-of-thumb column so it biases toward
sending more hands, not fewer). For context, the tool combines them
with the severity mix in the worst-hit areas to produce a line that
reads like "Deploy 24 adjusters × 5 days, 83% coverage" and flags a
shortfall if the window isn't long enough.

The questions I'd love your read on:
